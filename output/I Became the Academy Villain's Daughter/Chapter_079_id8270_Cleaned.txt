# 79 - A Strange Position

A hero represents justice.
One who has become a hero.
They must not commit evil deeds.
That's common sense even rookie hero parties understand.
That's why they fell into deeper anguish.

"Alpha. Can you defeat that troll?"

Alpha puffed up his arms, as thick as Destroyer's waist, and declared confidently.

"Three seconds is enough."
"Oh."
"Until my back breaks and I die."

What a useless bastard who's all talk.
Destroyer cursed inwardly.

"Destroyer. Can you trick the troll to go back the way we came, or hire mercenaries to fight together?"
"Impossible. Look at that troll bastard's gleaming eyes. If we try anything, he'll throw that rock he's holding to smash the carriage first."

Even Destroyer, who was inwardly criticizing others, was equally useless.
From the start, trolls are medium-sized monsters.
It would take a hundred armed soldiers poking it with ridiculously long 3-meter spears for three days and nights to barely kill one—an absurdly difficult extermination challenge.
And that calculation assumes there are knights or shield bearers tanking from the front.
Without proper tanking, those hundred spearmen would become a buffet for the troll to choose from.

"We'll just have to pick one and run through."
"Is that really okay?!"

Destroyer was honestly shocked by Nyarlathotep's cool response.

"Isn't there something about losing your qualification as a hero if you act unjustly?"
"Justice is relative. If we recklessly take responsibility we can't handle and cause even the coachman who could be saved to die with us, wouldn't that be the most unjust end?"

Nyarlathotep was a practical hero.

"Let's run over five people."
"Are you serious?"
"Horses are timid animals. If we try to save five people and only hit one by driving the carriage along the cliff path, they'll be too scared to run at full speed. Then we'll fail to meet the troll's demands."

Even Destroyer had to admit it was quite a sharp analysis.
Though questionable for a hero.

"If we try too hard to save a few people and everyone in our carriage dies too, then ultimately everyone here will die. Those who can survive should survive."

The companions who could preserve their lives thanks to this decision kept their mouths shut and followed the hero's decision.
About the terrible sensation of the carriage trampling people, I'll just say it's a memory I don't want to recall.
The troll kept its promise and let them pass safely.
After passing the fork in the road.
Nyarlathotep let out his characteristic laugh and said.

"We might have just met the smartest troll in the world."
"It's just a troll that asks crazy riddles."
"My, Destroyer. Haven't you noticed yet? The real reason that troll is so intelligent."

At that time, Destroyer didn't realize.
He just thought Nyarlathotep was an annoying guy.
What he had realized.
What he was trying to say.
Destroyer reached the same conclusion as Nyarlathotep only when he was retracing their adventure path alone after completing their journey.

"This was definitely the village."

When he visited the trading village looking for the coachman from his memory and coincidentally met the coachman who had experienced the world's smartest troll with him.
Destroyer exchanged awkward greetings with the coachman.

"I don't see your companions."
"Well, things turned out that way."
"Since we're connected by fate, would you like to travel together for a while?"
"You've got quite the courage."

Destroyer sat beside the coachman.
As they traveled aimlessly along the road.
He had three exchanges with the coachman and finally understood.
What Nyarlathotep had realized back then.


* *


"Now it's Oknodie's turn again. Imagine you're the veteran hero Destroyer and ask the coachman three questions."
"Destroyer really likes asking questions!"
"Otherwise it wouldn't be a problem."
"Did he really ask three questions every time?"
"Would I do that? If you keep being annoying, I'll count that as a question too."

As expected, Destroyer's adventure tales changed with each telling.
This time too, an interesting story emerged.

'Is the motif the trolley dilemma?'

A fairly famous thought experiment in ethics for modern people.
When faced with choices with clear trade-offs, where your decision could change a crisis threatening many lives to one threatening fewer lives.
Can you deliberately sacrifice fewer people by your own will, or would you stand by and let more people die? That's what the experiment asks.

'A troll conducting ethical experiments on humans, what a strange troll indeed!'

But just as there was a hint in the story of "The Sheep That Doesn't Decrease" from the first lecture, there must be a hint in this "Troll's Dilemma" too.
The three questions were just to retrace and confirm what I already suspected.

"Does the coachman's carriage have a spare wheel?"
"Good observation. Yes, you discover a spare wheel loaded on the back of the carriage."

That confirmed it.
The coachman wants to hide the fact that he faced the troll's dilemma.
Going so far as to make thorough preparations like having a spare wheel.

"My second question. Were there people who took the shortcut ahead of the carriage this time too?"
"The coachman would answer yes."
"My final question. Have the domain soldiers or the Adventurer's Guild ever attempted to exterminate the troll?"
"The coachman would answer with considerable confidence that not as far as he knows."
"That's enough. I think I get it now."
"Then try to guess."

It's not a particularly difficult problem.
If the hint is related to moral questioning, then the answer must be related to that too.

"The troll's ethical experiment ultimately made humans into 'accomplices.'"
"Righteous coachmen who tried to save their own kind, even at the risk of their own lives, attempted to hit just one person on the cliff path and all died."
"Conversely, selfish coachmen who coldly decided to run over the many on the wide road in order to survive were able to escape alive."

Destroyer nodded for me to continue.

"Actually, the troll's dilemma isn't at the fork in the road itself. The cliff path is a 'trap' and the main road is the 'answer.' The real dilemma comes afterward."
"Whether to report to the domain soldiers or guild that you cooperated with the troll's ethical experiment and ran over five people, risking being branded a murderer, or to pretend this bizarre accident never happened, keeping your mouth shut to prioritize your own safety."
"If you choose the former, the danger stationed near the trading village disappears, but your safety is threatened. If you choose the latter, the danger to the local community remains, but you stay safe."

Risk becoming an enemy of human society?
Or risk allowing humanity's enemy to continue existing?
The troll forces an absurdly intellectual dilemma.
And everyone who passed through this dilemma...
They're all coachmen who ran over the five people on the main road.
Those who chose selfishness over altruism toward their own kind are more likely to continue prioritizing their own safety rather than making decisions requiring self-sacrifice.
The surviving coachmen become accomplices and keep quiet about the danger that exists on the road they traveled.
They might even actively conceal and distort information about those who died.
Because if their crimes are revealed, they'll be in trouble!

"Former hero Nyarlathotep realized this fact instantly from the beginning of the adventure, right?"
"Correct. I'm curious though. How did you realize it so quickly?"
"Because Mr. Nyarlathotep's sense of justice differs from universal justice—it's a selfish justice!"

I can understand because I'm a player.
In any story, if the player dies, you can't witness what happens next.
That's why players with weak specs sometimes make choices they know are morally wrong or unjust.
I know this especially well because I played all sorts of random characters before I knew about rerolling.
Sometimes the weak must be cowardly and selfish to survive.
Nyarlathotep, though a hero, realized this and made a cold judgment.
Just like a "player" enjoying a game.
What an unusual NPC.

"If you had been born 15 years earlier and become Nyarlathotep's companion, you two would have gotten along very well."
"Ew. I wouldn't like that."
"You could have had the honor of being a hero party member?"
"That's only because I would have been weak. I'm strong now, you know?"

I flexed my arm with a grunt as if building muscles, and the professor looked at me like "this kid is cute" and said, "Yes, you're strong."

"Hm? Why is your face suddenly red? It's gross."
"It's not gross! It's just that these days, whenever I say I'm strong, everyone calls me an orc. You're the first person to react so calmly, professor."
"Huh."

Destroyer clicked his tongue and said.

"Tsundere student, troublesome."
"Oh come on, seriously!"

My grateful feelings completely disappeared!
