# Lecture 346 - Administrative Department Lecture on the Easy Way Out 2

"I'll begin the lecture."

Professor Stend Mill was a strict elderly professor, relatively old even among the many young faculty members at Gift Academy. He was a stubborn man whose wrinkles seemed to have absorbed the regrets accumulated over the years, with rigid lips that appeared to have hardened from forcibly clinging to beliefs to avoid facing those regrets. Students focused on his lecture, unable to dismiss him due to his age, impressed by his unyielding presence and dignity.

"Our current society is maintained by the gifts that goddesses have granted to humanity. Those with the talent of a swordsman develop sword-related skills three times faster, while those with the talent of a farmer develop farming-related skills three times faster."

Talent.
For a player like me who wants to develop all kinds of skills, it's sometimes an obstacle, though occasionally beneficial when my talents align with my goals, creating explosive growth. But for NPCs living their one and only life, talent is an inescapable burden.

"If there's someone here with a farmer's talent who wants to become a swordsman, the world would say: You don't have the talent for swordsmanship. Don't waste your natural gifts."
"..."
"Everyone has experienced this at least once. When what you want to do differs from what you can do. When you find yourself at a crossroads with multiple life choices."

The elderly professor maintained a brief silence, as if guaranteeing the students time for their faint contemplations and imaginings.
Thump.
The brief moment of reflection granted to them scattered with the professor's palm striking the podium.

"If you were swayed by my story, you've been deceived at least once by family, acquaintances, or society."
"!?"
"Farmer's talent? Truly, less than 1% of all farmers actually possess such talent. Yet those who are told they have a farmer's talent exceed 10% of actual farmers. First student to answer why gets extra credit."

Mesugakki raised her hand immediately.

"Is it because they package someone's future under the name of 'talent' to force them into an expected career path, Professor~?"
"Correct. Extra credit for student Mesugakki."

Oknodie, who typically allowed first-time novice students to answer questions at least once, glared at Princess Mesugakki for getting the answer in one try, thinking she was annoying.
Princess Mesugakki stuck out her tongue to tease her. It was a long, crimson, irritating tongue.

"The Empire doesn't want the working class to rise to the warrior class. So they constantly claim: Adventure is arduous and difficult. Monsters are strong and outsiders are frightening."
"They want you to hold a plow instead of a sword. They create a culture where anyone trying to become an adventurer is ridiculed. If you don't follow the path society has set for you, you become a laughingstock."
"And for those who still try to deviate from that path, they make it difficult to even begin by mandating 'Educational Qualification Completion' at specific times each year and collecting 'Qualification Exam Fees.'"

Making it difficult not only for students but also for teachers to teach easily.
This is how swordsmanship becomes distant from people.

"Unless you have truly passionate instructors and students, teaching and learning swordsmanship becomes rare. Moreover, instructors who have spent time and money taking exams and paying exam fees must charge high tuition unless they're extremely wealthy or running a charity."

"When consumption is forced, learning comes with a price tag. While the poor struggle to visually copy a single movement, the wealthy physically master an entire swordsmanship technique. This is why it's easier for the upper class to become swordsmen, but difficult for the lower class."

It's easy for someone at the top of the class pyramid to learn lower-class skills, but difficult for someone at the bottom to learn upper-class skills.

"Through institutionalized techniques and legally enforced restrictions, a country can artificially control the number of swordsmen it produces. This is the background that gave birth to the 'Skill Training Institutes' established by the Empire to reduce revolutionary warriors during an era when countless farmers were joining the 'Revolutionary Army.'"

The students felt an inexplicable solemnity.
It was the solemnity that comes from facing a wrongful reality, feeling helpless without the means or courage to correct it.
So what?
Why should I know about lower-class difficulties?
What does that have to do with me?
More than half of the noble students had arrogant looks, but Professor Stend Mill didn't point this out.

"In the Skill Monopoly Theory lecture, you'll learn what systems society has created to monopolize certain skills, and what the expected effects and results are."
"Learning this won't dramatically change your lives immediately. But I guarantee it will change one aspect of how you view the world."
"Including realizing how extraordinary the learning opportunity you're currently enjoying truly is."

Students occasionally yawned during Stend Mill's unusually theory-heavy lecture at Gift Academy, where practical education typically dominates.
However, not only heavyweight first-years like Oknodie and Princess Mesugakki, but also notable figures like Prince Hector, the Dark Knight Mob, and Jaku with his dark mana, all paid attention.
Except for the two who truly knew why they needed to focus, the others were merely attempting to follow along with whatever these two were concentrating on.
At least the academic atmosphere was excellent.
Professor Stend Mill finished his lecture without a single practical exercise.

"Not really different from last week?"
"We reviewed for Oknodie and the new students."
"Still boring when we don't use our bodies."

Mob also expressed doubts among the dissatisfied students.

"Oknodie, why are you taking this class?"
"Because there's a skill I want to learn!"
"A skill...? Does this lecture eventually teach skills too?"
"Well, there's a limit to how many people can learn it!"

Skill Monopoly Theory.
Why monopoly, of all things?
Mob had an ominous feeling.
It was the effect of his developed "Danger Sense" since he started receiving training from Oknodie and ended up wearing full armor with a bikini armor on top.
This danger warning was even stronger than when he had to wear the bikini armor.
Not as bad as the hard labor on the cruise ship, but too dangerous for a crisis within the Academy!

"Is it okay if I keep taking this class?"
"It doesn't matter! It's not physically demanding."
"Phew."
"But remember this."
"What?"
"The Administrative Department has gentle professors like Master Myungho, but also landmine professors like Professor Platon!"
"!!!"

Oh no.
He had completely forgotten.
Professor Platon, infamous for his "Advanced Class Physical Enhancement" lecture, the most notorious professor of the first semester.
Even students not in the advanced class felt it. After each lecture, they would see advanced class students barely making their way to the lounge or dormitory with trembling legs.
He must be an insanely tough professor!
Even the Knight Department professors weren't that harsh. It was natural to fear a professor who wrings out human strength like squeezing a wet towel.
Thinking that he was essentially taking a class with such a terrifying professor made him feel afraid even though he hadn't been before.
...Is it really okay for me to take this?

"With that mindset, you'd be better off dropping the course."
"Ugh. Aren't you scared at all, Jaku?"

After relaying what he heard from Oknodie, Jaku snorted.

"If it's not physically demanding, then it must be mentally challenging. When it comes to monopoly, there will be a struggle between those trying to protect limited resources and those trying to take them."
"Oh."
"Most likely, the class will involve applying what we learn to compete for skill monopolization, with students who successfully monopolize or maintain monopoly for extended periods earning points for midterms and finals."

Mob honestly admired him.

"You're really smart, aren't you? Figuring all that out after just one lecture."
"This is nothing. As the professor said..."
"?"
"...Never mind. There's no point telling you."
"Come on, tell me."

Despite Mob's urging, Jaku kept his mouth shut.
The true talented top 1%.
The top 10% who are evaluated as having talent in lower-class skills despite not actually having it.
The 1% belongs to beings like Oknodie.
The 10% belongs to beings like Mob.
He himself belongs to neither, part of the remaining 90%.
Even if the state declares he has no talent, he can't help but feel the gap between those with real talent and those without—he has a dull, untalented body.
But if even someone as dull as himself could rise to a position to monopolize skills and make them his own, he could prevent those stronger than him from wielding a sword.
A lightning realization flashed through Jaku's mind.
Isn't that the true meaning of skill monopoly?
A method for the untalented to defeat geniuses.
No genius in the world can flourish if they're not even aware of their talent and have no opportunity to use it.

'The concept of swordsmanship training institutes certainly spread from the Empire to various kingdoms.'

Starting as mere institutes, they evolve into full-fledged swordsman guilds once they begin producing promising swordsmen.
Swordsmen with strength far superior to adventurers who swing swords recklessly.
Thus, the path of swordsmanship is reserved only for the upper class who can afford expensive tuition.

'What are the rulers of the Empire and kingdoms so afraid of that they strive so hard to monopolize skills?'

Are they afraid of top 1% geniuses like Oknodie?
When your eyes open, you see a different landscape.
Then what is Gift Academy?
A sanctuary for talented children who forcibly overcome the educational barriers built by the Empire and kingdoms.
This is... practically a den of heretics defying the monopoly of the Empire and kingdoms.

'The Empire... the Academy... the Foundation...'

Jaku's deepening thoughts were interrupted by a paper airplane hitting his head.

"Sorry, first-year! Could you pick that up and throw it back?"

Jaku picked up the airplane with a sense of regret.
Then he unfolded the paper airplane and signed the signature line with a pen he took out from his chest.

"!?"

Bellocasio, who had thrown the paper airplane, grinned.

"Finally caught one. A useful first-year slave."
